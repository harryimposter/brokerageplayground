Goal Derivation

How a client's Preservation / Income / Growth goal is inferred from the balance sheet — methodology, formula, and a worked example for every client

The one-line idea. The old model assumed the client handed you a target split. Real clients don't. So GOALS.deriveGoals(client) infers a three-bucket goal — Preservation · Income · Growth — from both sides of the balance sheet plus the client's revealed risk appetite, never from what they own for its own sake. Every number below was produced by running the actual engine (goals.js + PARAMS) against the real seed book. They are exact, not illustrative.

1  ·  The reframe — “have” vs “need”

The engine separates two things the old goals.target conflated:

  • Current allocation (“have”) — what the book holds today. Read straight off positions.
  • Goal allocation (“need”) — what the book should hold, given the client's obligations, horizon and appetite. This is what deriveGoals computes — and what replaces the hand-typed target.

The methodology is liability-driven / goals-based (after Chhabra's risk-allocation framework, where the Safety / Market / Aspirational buckets map onto Preservation / Income / Growth). The principle: fund the obligations first, then deploy the surplus by how hard the money must work.

1.1   The evidence ladder — the source field

The inference uses the strongest evidence available and records which tier it leaned on, in priority order:

sourceHas a $ goal?Has a risk descriptor?What it leans onConfidence
stated-goal✓ → required returnusually The funding number (objective — how hard the money must work) + willingness0.90
stated-risk✗✓ → willingness The written appetite (“aggressive” / “conservative”) blended with the book0.70
revealed✗✗ The portfolio alone — there is no form to read off0.55

A goal is a number (“grow to $200m by 2045”) → it gives a required return. A risk descriptor is temperament in words (“aggressive”) → it gives willingness, but says nothing about the target. stated-risk = appetite known, goal unknown.

2  ·  The formula

Each of the three buckets gets a raw score built from measured drivers; the three are then normalised to 100%. Every input is a real quantity off the balance sheet; every coefficient lives in PARAMS and is a calibration choice.

2.1   Willingness — read two ways, then blended

Willingness (0–1) is the risk-appetite knob. It is read from the current book (revealed) and, if present, from the free-text risk descriptor (stated):

Revealed = 0.5·R + 0.3·D + 0.2·C R = risk-asset share (Equity + Alternatives, as a fraction of the book) D = low-defensiveness = 1 − min(1, defensiveShare / 0.55) defensive = Cash + Fixed Income + Commodity C = concentration appetite = min(1, topSingleName / 0.25) (index cores / fundless sleeves excluded) Stated = aggressive→0.95 · growth→0.80 · moderate→0.50 · conservative/income→0.30 · (none)→null Willingness = (Stated is null) ? Revealed : 0.5·Stated + 0.5·Revealed

2.2   The three raw scores

caution = 1 − Willingness // willingness is BIDIRECTIONAL: low appetite adds ballast PROTECTION = 8 + 1.0·nearBill% + 0.3·debtLoad% + max(0, topName%−15) + 15·[drawdown] + 3·max(0, 5−H) + 28·caution INCOME = 8 + 8·incomeNeed% + 8·service% + 0.6·debtLoad% + 15·[drawdown] + 18·caution GROWTH = 8 + 5·requiredReturn% + 3·max(0, H−6) + Wᵥ·Willingness Wᵥ = 30 if a funding goal exists, else 45 (lean harder on appetite when no goal) vector = normalise( Preservation, Income, Growth ) → sums to 100
SymbolMeaning (all measured off the client)Drives
nearBill%One-off bills due soon (tax, bridge), % of AUMPreservation
debtLoad%Total liabilities, % of AUMIncome (match) + Preservation (ballast)
service%Annual debt servicing (balance × ~6%), % of AUMIncome
incomeNeed%Stated annual spending draw, % of AUMIncome
topName%Largest single-name position (index cores excluded)Preservation (above 15%)
HHorizon midpoint, years; [drawdown] = spend-down phaseGrowth (long) / Preservation (short, drawdown)
requiredReturn%CAGR needed to hit the funding goal: (target/base)^(1/yrs) − 1Growth
Why willingness is bidirectional. Originally willingness only fed Growth, so a cautious client with no liabilities or income needs still came out growth-heavy (Preservation/Income only ever got weight from needs). The caution = 1 − willingness term routes a low appetite into ballast: full-aggressive pushes ~45 points into Growth, full-cautious pushes ~46 (28+18) into Preservation+Income — a balanced two-way dial.

2.3   Anti-circularity

The book sets the willingness knob (a scalar), not the target vector. The vector is always rebuilt by the formula, so the gap between “have” and “need” stays meaningful — the engine never trivially concludes “you already own the right thing.” A manual client.goalOverride pins a vector and short-circuits the model.

3  ·  Worked examples — every client

Each block shows the measured inputs, the willingness build, the three raw scores term-by-term, and the normalised goal. Numbers are exact from goals.js.

Fable  stated-goal · Professional · $38.0m · “Aggressive” · horizon 10y

Funding: “Grow to $75m by 2034”. AI-concentrated book — 88% equity, a 24% single name.

Willingness = 0.5·0.95 (stated) + 0.5·[0.5·0.88(R) + 0.3·0.891(D) + 0.2·0.96(C) = 0.899] = 0.925

requiredReturn = (75/38)^(1/8) − 1 = 8.87%/yr PROTECTION = 8 + 9.0 (24%−15 conc) + 2.1 (caution 28·0.075) = 19.1 INCOME = 8 + 1.35 (caution 18·0.075) = 9.4 GROWTH = 8 + 44.35 (5·8.87) + 12 (3·[10−6]) + 27.75 (30·0.925) = 92.1 → normalise(19.1, 9.4, 92.1)

→ Preservation 16 · Income 8 · Growth 76

High required return (8.9%) and high appetite drive Growth; the 24% single name is the only real pull toward Preservation.
